I am an Officer on the Data Analytics team at the Development Research Initiative (dRi) in Dhaka. Most of my work is survey research, and I take part in every stage of it. I contribute on making survey questionnaires, turn them into digital forms in SurveyCTO, KoboToolbox, and ODK, keep an eye on data quality while the survey is still in the field, clean the large and often messy datasets that come back, and run the analysis at the end. The studies I work on are carried out with national and international partners such as the World Bank, the University of Oxford, MIT, LSE, Purdue, and BRAC.

Working with these teams every day taught me to write code that is clean and easy to run again, so a colleague, or my own future self, can reuse it without guesswork. That habit led me to build my own tools. I wrote a set of Stata programs that take the slow, repetitive parts out of survey data cleaning, and I built YourRA, a website that transcribes and translates recorded interviews for qualitative research such as in-depth interviews, focus group discussions, and case studies.

The same data I clean for work also pulls me toward my own questions. Using the Bangladesh Demographic and Health Survey (BDHS), I study how families find and pay for health care, and how this is changing as private hospitals and clinics spread. My papers ask why so many births in Bangladesh now happen by caesarean section, how depression after childbirth is tied to a mother's income and education, how a woman's control over her own reproductive choices affects her mental health, and where patients with high blood pressure slip out of care.

I came to this work through a Bachelor's degree in Statistics from Government Titumir College, affiliated with the University of Dhaka, and an early research internship at the BRAC James P. Grant School of Public Health, where I first saw how much careful data can tell us about people's health. That mix of hands-on survey work and my own research is what I enjoy most, and what I want to keep building on.

Research interest

I am drawn to a simple question: how do policies and programmes actually change people's lives? I want to answer it in low and lower middle income countries, using carefully designed surveys and field experiments, and I keep coming back to public health, health economics, and labour.

  • Impact evaluation. Experimental and quasi-experimental studies, such as randomised trials, difference in differences, instrumental variables, and regression discontinuity, to find out whether a policy or programme really works.
  • Public health. Mother and child health, mental health, and the growing burden of long term illness, studied with large national health surveys.
  • Health economics. How people get and pay for care, the place of private hospitals and out of pocket cost, and the gap in access between richer and poorer households.
  • Labour and development. How work, income, and opportunity shape the wellbeing of families in low and lower middle income countries.
  • Biostatistics and measurement. Survey design, regression and multilevel models for health data, and careful, reproducible analysis.

  1. 1
    From medical necessity to private sector dominance: a two decade evolution of caesarean section determinants in Bangladesh (2004 to 2022)
    Rony, A. R., Hossen, A., Asha, A. S., and Nahin, K. S. A.
    Scientific Reports (Nature Portfolio), 2026.
    Published DOI Journal page
    Caesarean section (CS) use in Bangladesh has climbed faster than in almost any comparable country, yet whether this reflects clinical need or supply side incentives has stayed unclear. Pooling three periods of the Bangladesh Demographic and Health Survey covering 26,845 ever married women (2004 to 2007, 2011 to 2014, and 2018 to 2022), we model how the determinants of CS changed over twenty years. Weighted CS prevalence rose from 6.8 percent in 2004 to 2007 to 39.0 percent in 2018 to 2022. The pull of genuine medical indications weakened over the same window: the adjusted odds of CS for multiple births fell from 9.55 to 2.93, consistent with surgery spreading well past clinically necessary cases. The socioeconomic gradient reversed as well, with women in the poorer wealth quintile moving from lower odds of CS (adjusted OR 0.53) to higher odds (adjusted OR 1.45) as private facilities expanded into lower income groups. We read these patterns as a shift from medical necessity toward private sector dominance, and argue for stronger price and quality regulation, routine clinical audit, and investment in midwifery led care to bring CS use back into line with need.

  1. 2
    Reproductive coercion as a determinant of anxiety and depression among married women in Bangladesh: a nationally representative survey weighted multinomial analysis of the 2022 DHS
    Nahin, K. S. A., Hossen, A., Asha, A. S., Islam, T., and Rony, A. R.
    Under review, 2026. Preprint available.
    Preprint and DOI
    Anxiety and depression often occur together, yet they are usually studied one at a time, which hides the women who carry both. Using the 2022 Bangladesh Demographic and Health Survey (20,029 ever married women), we treat mental health as a single four way outcome, neither condition, anxiety only, depression only, or both, measured with the validated GAD-7 and PHQ-9 scales, and fit a survey weighted multinomial model. Our focus is reproductive coercion and the wider lack of decision making power in the home, and we ask how these forms of control relate to each mental health state once standard social and economic factors are held constant. About 15 percent of women reported anxiety alone and 4.2 percent reported both conditions together. By modelling the comorbid case directly, the study shows that control over a woman's reproductive choices is linked to the heaviest mental health burden, and argues for screening and support that treat anxiety and depression as a connected problem.
  2. 3
    Socioeconomic inequality and postpartum depression in Bangladesh: evidence from BDHS 2022
    Islam, T., Rony, A. R., Nahin, K. S. A., Asha, A. S., Hossen, A., and Hossain, M. G.
    Under review, 2026. Preprint available.
    Preprint and DOI
    Postpartum depression carries lasting costs for both mother and child, but its social distribution in Bangladesh has not been described at the national level. Using the 2022 Bangladesh Demographic and Health Survey, we estimate the prevalence of postpartum depressive symptoms among recently delivered women and ask how that burden is shared across socioeconomic position. We summarise inequality with concentration indices and decompose it into its drivers, including household wealth, maternal education, place of residence, and contact with antenatal and delivery care, within a survey weighted regression framework. By identifying which modifiable factors explain most of the gap, the study points to where screening and support would do the most to reach the women who carry the heaviest and least recognised burden.
  3. 4
    The hypertension care cascade in Bangladesh: a nationally representative analysis of BDHS 2022
    Nahin, K. S. A., Rony, A. R., Tasnim, S. J., Islam, T., Hossen, A., and Asha, A. S.
    Under review, 2026. Preprint available.
    Preprint and DOI
    Hypertension is now a leading driver of cardiovascular disease in Bangladesh, yet most affected adults never reach effective control. Using nationally representative blood pressure measurements and self reported treatment from the 2022 Bangladesh Demographic and Health Survey, we build the hypertension care cascade and estimate the share of adults with hypertension who are screened, diagnosed, treated, and finally controlled. We locate the largest losses along the cascade and use multivariable models to find the individual and household characteristics linked to progression at each stage. By naming the weakest links, from awareness through to control, the analysis points to concrete places where chronic disease care could be strengthened within the current health system.

Research software

Each of these tools started the same way: a job in a real project was taking too long or going wrong by hand, so I built something to fix it. They now save time on every study I touch.

A Stata toolkit for survey data cleaning Stata commands
The problem

Three cleaning tasks come up on almost every survey project, and each is slow and error prone when done by hand.

What I built

Three Stata programs, each for one of those jobs.

  • recode_oth, recode_nrep, recode_rep: people often answer through an "Other, specify" field, and those answers need to be moved back into the main choice list when they come up often. Enumerators also sometimes type an answer that is already a choice. Once the open answers are translated, these programs recode them into the main variable automatically, based on how often each answer appears, with no manual cleaning.
  • exprep: surveys often repeat a block of questions, for example the same questions for each disease a person reports, which makes the data hard to work with. exprep turns it into a simple layout and builds one set of variables per item, so 27 diseases become 27 clean sets of variables.
  • multisplit: when you correct a "select all that apply" answer, you also have to fix its separate yes or no columns by hand, which is repetitive. multisplit rebuilds those columns from the corrected main variable and keeps the labels intact.
Why it matters

This work used to be slow and easy to get wrong. Now it runs from a script and saves a lot of time.

YourRA, Bangla transcription for qualitative research web app, generative AI
The problem

Qualitative research runs on hours of recorded interviews, and turning them into usable text is slow and costly. Done by hand, a research assistant needs about a day for one hour of audio.

What it does

YourRA is a website where a researcher uploads hours of audio at once and gets back a ready Word document. It gives a Bangla verbatim transcript with an English translation, automatic speaker labels for moderators and participants, timestamps, and a respondent demographic table. It accepts common formats (MP3, WAV, M4A, OGG, MP4). It is built for sensitive research: files are encrypted in transit and at rest, uploads are never used to train AI models, and the researcher chooses when the audio is deleted (automatically within one to seven days). At about 399 taka per hour, it costs far less than manual transcription.

Why it matters

A job that took about a day per hour of audio now takes a small fraction of the time and cost, while the researcher still checks the result. It is made for in-depth interviews, focus group discussions, key informant interviews, and case studies.

A study area map generator for Bangladesh R, Shiny
The problem

Field teams, survey managers, and students often need clean, publication ready study area maps but do not have the GIS training to make them, and producing each map by hand is slow.

What it does

An interactive R Shiny application built on Leaflet, terra, elevatr, and GADM boundaries. Users pick administrative units from national level down to upazila, and the app draws the map with automatic legends, elevation shading, and customisable colour palettes, then exports a high resolution image.

Why it matters

Someone with no GIS background can produce a report ready map in a few minutes, which removes a common bottleneck in preparing field plans and study documentation.
